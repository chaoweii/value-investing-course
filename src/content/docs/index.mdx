## Begin with the ownership claim

Before making the first decision, spend a short session learning [what one share actually gives
you](/ownership/). You will see why shareholders do not directly own company cash, why common
equity is paid last, and why limited liability protects you from company debt but not from loss.

## The learning loop

1. **Understand the claim.** Know your rights, limitations, and place in the capital stack.
2. **Decide before theory.** Record what you believe and what would falsify it.
3. **Learn the owner framework.** Rebuild earnings, moat, reinvestment, and valuation from first principles.
4. **Understand the price of money.** Learn how Treasury yields alter businesses, valuations, and opportunity cost.
5. **Return to the same decision.** Identify exactly why your judgment changed—or why it did not.
6. **Repeat across business archetypes.** Learn which tools transfer and which do not.
7. **Construct the portfolio.** Let strong business outcomes matter without allowing one error to end the process.
8. **Understand the market machine.** Separate business value from the flows and constraints that
   discover today's price.

## The bridge most investors skip

Business quality determines the cash flows you may receive. The price of money determines what
those uncertain future cash flows are worth today. The [Macro Backbone](/macro/) teaches this
connection without turning value investing into short-term rate forecasting.

Toast is the course's main longitudinal case. Begin with a restaurant owner's product experience,
then follow the same company through [profit inflection](/core/product-scale-to-owner-value/),
[SBC and buybacks](/core/owner-fcf-per-share/), and the [full DCF build](/core/tost-dcf-lab/).
The purpose is not to promote the stock. It is to learn why product conviction, business quality,
per-share economics, and price are four separate gates.

The final [Portfolio Construction Capstone](/portfolio/) converts the underwriting into a
long-term allocation policy: evidence-driven, probabilistic, tax-free, and deliberately resistant
to trading noise.

The final [Market Machine phase](/market/) explains how that policy meets real exchanges, ETFs,
index rules, liquidity, and forced flows. Fundamentals determine long-term owner value; market
structure determines which buyer and seller set today's price.
