## The frameworks disagree for useful reasons

| Approach | Best use | Failure mode |
|---|---|---|
| Quality concentration | Exceptional economics, high evidence quality, conservative price | Quality becomes an excuse for overpaying |
| Anti-ruin inversion | Large positions and uncertain external risks | Excess caution prevents winners from mattering |
| Downside risk budget | Cyclical, correlated, or governance-sensitive assets | Static limits ignore improving evidence |
| Press and cut | A visible inflection with rapidly improving evidence | Activity and price action replace underwriting |
| Explicit probability valuation | Transparent scenario comparison | False precision in probabilities |
| Simple conviction tiers | Operationally robust decision making | Broad labels hide material differences |

## Recommended synthesis

Begin with business-quality asymmetry. Use explicit scenarios to understand payoff. Then size at
the **lowest** amount permitted by conviction, evidence quality, permanent-loss budget, theme
concentration, governance, and liquidity.

Fractional Kelly can illustrate how payoff and probability interact. It should not override the
fact that your probability estimates are uncertain and correlations change during stress.
