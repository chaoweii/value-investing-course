## The final portfolio lesson is patience

Stocks are long-term ownership. Cash and Treasury bills are not a failure of imagination; they are
the option to wait for better odds. Valuation changes expected return, cycles create opportunity,
and patience prevents forced mistakes.

That sounds simple. It is emotionally hard because cycles do not arrive on schedule. An expensive
market can become more expensive. A cheap market can become cheaper. A patient investor may look
wrong for years before the odds show up in returns.

```text
Stocks are long-term ownership.
Cash/T-bills are the option to wait.
Valuation changes expected return.
Cycles create opportunity.
Patience prevents forced mistakes.
```

## Buffett, Munger, and Marks are not saying the same thing as market timers

**Buffett:** wait for understandable opportunities with favorable odds. The edge is not predicting
next month; it is refusing to buy mediocre odds when cash has a respectable risk-free return.

**Munger:** avoid envy, activity, and false precision. A rule that makes you trade because it looks
smart is dangerous if it weakens judgment.

**Howard Marks:** cycles do not tell you exactly when to trade, but they do tell you where risk and
reward are leaning. When optimism is extreme, future return usually has less room. When fear is
extreme, future return usually has more room.

The simulator below tests that philosophy using history. It compares buy-and-hold S&P 500 against
simple annual rules that move between S&P 500 exposure and Treasury money-market returns inside a
tax-free account.

## What the simulator is allowed to do

The simulator uses:

- Robert Shiller's long-run S&P 500 price, dividends, earnings, CPI, and CAPE history;
- FRED 3-month Treasury bill returns and long Treasury yields where available;
- annual rebalancing;
- reinvested dividends and reinvested Treasury yield;
- no leverage, no shorting, and no tax drag.

The most important guardrail: each year’s allocation uses the prior year’s valuation and yield
regime. It is not allowed to peek at the return that is about to happen.

## How to read the result

Do not ask, “Which rule had the highest historical ending wealth?” That is how backtests become
dangerous.

Ask better questions:

1. Did the rule reduce permanent-damage risk, or only reduce volatility?
2. Did the rule rely on staying out of equities for too long?
3. Did it underperform buy-and-hold for emotionally painful periods?
4. Did it preserve enough upside when valuations were high?
5. Would you actually follow it when it looked foolish?

<ExerciseReveal prompt="If the valuation-aware rule underperforms buy-and-hold for five years, does that prove it is wrong?">
Not automatically. A patient rule can look wrong during the final stage of a bull market. But you
must ask whether the rule's mechanism still makes sense, whether the opportunity cost of cash has
changed, and whether the rule has become an excuse for avoiding ownership altogether.
</ExerciseReveal>

## The cycle lesson

The phrase “this time is different” is sometimes partly true. Technology changes. Regulation
changes. Monetary policy regimes change. But investor behavior, credit cycles, and valuation
gravity have not disappeared.

The right lesson is not “sell every high P/E market.” The right lesson is:

- valuation is weak as a one-year timing signal;
- valuation is useful as a long-horizon expected-return signal;
- cash has value when it lets you avoid forced selling and exploit future fear;
- a rule that cannot survive years of embarrassment is not a real rule.

<ChineseNote title="周期的边界">
周期会重复，但不会按你的时间表重复。估值纪律不是预测明天，而是提高未来十年的赔率。
</ChineseNote>
