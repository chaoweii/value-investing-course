## The decomposition

A useful first-principles approximation is:

```text
Long-term nominal Treasury yield
≈ expected path of real short rates
+ expected inflation
+ term premium
```

The **term premium** is the extra compensation investors demand for locking money away while
inflation, policy, fiscal supply, and market conditions can change.

## The Fed controls a point, not the entire curve

The Federal Reserve strongly influences overnight rates. A ten-year yield reflects the expected
average path of future short rates plus inflation and term-premium uncertainty. It can therefore
rise after a Fed cut if investors simultaneously expect stronger growth, more inflation, or a
larger term premium.

| Force | Typical upward pressure on long yields | Important complication |
|---|---|---|
| Inflation | Higher expected loss of purchasing power | Credible disinflation can lower yields before actual inflation falls |
| Real growth | Stronger demand for capital and a higher expected policy path | Productivity growth can improve corporate cash flows too |
| Fiscal supply | More duration must be absorbed by investors | Supply matters through demand, maturity mix, and confidence |
| QE / QT | Central-bank buying removes duration; QT returns it | Expectations often move yields before balance sheets change |
| Global demand | Reserve managers, pensions, banks, and hedged buyers affect demand | Currency-hedging costs can reverse apparent yield advantages |
| Risk appetite | Flight-to-safety demand can lower Treasury yields | Credit spreads may widen even as Treasury yields fall |

## Balance-sheet policy is a separate force

**QE**, or quantitative easing, means the Fed buys securities and usually expands reserves in the
banking system. **QT**, or quantitative tightening, means the Fed lets securities run off and
shrinks its asset holdings.

QE and QT can influence long yields by changing the supply of duration private investors must hold.
They can also influence liquidity by changing reserve balances. But they do not mechanically set
the 10-year Treasury yield. The long end still prices expected short rates, inflation, growth,
term premium, fiscal supply, and global demand.

For the plumbing details, continue to [Fed Plumbing · Rates, Reserves, And The Balance Sheet](/macro/fed-plumbing/).

## Read the curve as a set of disagreements

- The front end is heavily shaped by the expected near-term Fed path.
- Intermediate maturities combine policy expectations with growth and inflation beliefs.
- The long end carries more duration, inflation uncertainty, fiscal-supply risk, and term premium.
- Credit and mortgage spreads reveal risks that the Treasury curve alone cannot show.
