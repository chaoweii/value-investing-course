## Begin with the contract

A Treasury is a contract: you pay a price today and receive specified dollars later.
The **yield** is the annualized return implied by that price and those promised cash flows.

For a one-year zero-coupon bond:

```text
Price today = $100 / (1 + yield)
```

If the required yield rises from 4% to 5%, the same promised `$100` must be purchased for a
lower price. **Bond prices and yields move in opposite directions because the future dollars
do not change when the market’s required return changes.**

## Maturity is time; duration is sensitivity

Maturity tells you when principal is repaid. Duration estimates how sensitive price is to a
change in yield:

```text
Approximate bond price change ≈ −duration × change in yield
```

An eight-duration bond loses roughly 8% when yields rise by one percentage point. A business
whose value depends mostly on cash flows far in the future behaves like a long-duration asset,
even when it has no debt.

“公司没有债务，所以不怕加息”是不完整的。没有债务只降低经营融资风险；如果大部分价值来自遥远未来，估值仍然高度依赖利率。
</ChineseNote>

## Nominal yield versus real yield

Nominal yield tells you how many dollars you expect to receive. Real yield asks how much
purchasing power those dollars may retain:

```text
Approximate real yield ≈ nominal yield − expected inflation
```

A 5% nominal yield with 3% expected inflation offers roughly 2% expected real return. For an
equity investor, this real return is a serious alternative: the stock should compensate you
for uncertain cash flows, permanent-loss risk, and analytical effort.

## Why Treasuries become opportunity cost

Treasuries do not make equities unattractive mechanically. They change the hurdle.

- When Treasury yields rise, investors can earn more without underwriting a company.
- Equity valuations generally require either lower prices, stronger cash-flow growth, or both.
- When yields fall, distant cash flows become more valuable, but the reason for the decline
  matters. A recessionary yield collapse can harm the business while helping the valuation math.
