## Start with the simplest question

When people say the **Federal Reserve**, or **Fed**, “prints money,” they usually do not mean a
printing press making paper bills. They usually mean the Fed creates **bank reserves**.

A **bank reserve** is an electronic deposit that a bank holds at the Fed. It is money for banks.
It is not the same as your checking-account deposit. You cannot directly spend bank reserves at a
restaurant, but banks use reserves to settle payments with each other.

A **bank deposit** is your claim on a bank. If your brokerage account or checking account shows
cash, that is usually a deposit or money-market claim. A deposit is your asset, but it is the
bank's liability.

That distinction is the first key:

```text
Bank reserves = money between banks and the Fed.
Bank deposits = money customers see in bank and brokerage accounts.
```

准备金只存在于银行和美联储之间。普通人看到的是银行存款、现金、货币基金份额或证券，不是准备金本身。
</ChineseNote>

## The two knobs

The Fed has two different policy knobs.

**The price knob** controls the price of overnight money. The Fed directly sets administered rates
such as **IORB**, the interest paid to banks on reserve balances, and **ON RRP**, the overnight
reverse repo rate available to eligible money-market investors.

**The quantity knob** controls the size and composition of the Fed's own assets. The Fed can own
more or fewer Treasury securities and agency mortgage-backed securities, called **MBS**. When it
buys securities, its balance sheet expands. When it lets securities mature without replacement,
its balance sheet shrinks.

The nuance: the Fed can directly choose how many securities it buys or lets run off. It does not
perfectly choose the exact level of bank reserves every day. Reserves are the residual after other
Fed liabilities move.

```text
Fed assets
≈ bank reserves
+ currency in circulation
+ Treasury General Account
+ ON RRP
+ other Fed liabilities and capital
```

The **Treasury General Account**, or **TGA**, is the U.S. Treasury's checking account at the Fed.
When the Treasury builds that account, cash leaves the private banking system. **Currency in
circulation** is paper cash held outside the Fed. Paper currency enters circulation when banks
request physical cash from the Fed for customers.

## How the Fed injects liquidity

**Liquidity** means the ability to pay, borrow, or trade without large damage. The Fed can inject
liquidity mainly through asset purchases or temporary lending.

### QE: permanent-ish reserve creation

**QE**, or quantitative easing, means the Fed buys Treasury securities or agency MBS.

```text
Fed buys Treasury/MBS from a dealer or investor
→ Fed credits the reserve account of the seller's bank
→ seller receives a bank deposit
→ Fed assets rise
→ banking-system reserves usually rise
```

Example:

| Step | Fed balance sheet | Banking system |
|---|---|---|
| Fed buys `$100B` of Treasuries | Assets `+$100B` securities | Seller's bank reserves `+$100B` |
| Seller receives payment | Liabilities `+$100B` reserves | Seller deposit `+$100B` |

This is why QE is called “money printing,” but the exact words matter. The Fed created reserves;
the seller received a deposit because the banking system credited the seller's account.

### Repo: temporary reserve injection

A **repo**, short for repurchase agreement, is a cash loan secured by collateral. In a Fed repo
operation:

```text
Fed lends cash/reserves overnight
→ dealer pledges Treasury collateral
→ reserves rise temporarily
→ trade reverses when the repo matures
```

Repo is more like a short-term liquidity bridge than a permanent QE purchase. The Fed gets its
cash back when the repo unwinds.

## How the Fed pulls liquidity back

The Fed can pull liquidity back through balance-sheet runoff or reverse repo facilities.

### QT: securities run off

**QT**, or quantitative tightening, means the Fed lets securities mature without fully replacing
them.

```text
Fed-owned bond matures
→ Treasury repays the Fed
→ Fed does not reinvest
→ Fed asset disappears
→ a matching liability, often reserves or ON RRP, declines
```

QT is usually not the Fed dumping bonds into the market. It is more often passive runoff. But QT
does not always drain bank reserves one-for-one because other Fed liabilities also move.

Simplified reserve shortcut:

```text
Change in reserves
≈ change in Fed securities
- change in TGA
- change in currency
- change in ON RRP
```

If the Fed securities portfolio falls by `$60B`, ON RRP falls by `$40B`, TGA rises by `$30B`, and
currency rises by `$10B`, then:

```text
-$60B securities runoff
- $30B TGA increase
- $10B currency increase
- (-$40B ON RRP decline)
= -$60B reserve change
```

The Fed balance sheet shrank by `$60B`, but the reserve effect depended on the other plumbing.

### ON RRP: temporary cash drain from nonbanks

**ON RRP**, the overnight reverse repurchase agreement facility, lets eligible money-market funds
and other counterparties place cash at the Fed overnight.

```text
Money-market fund places cash at Fed
→ private-market cash is parked at the Fed overnight
→ Fed provides Treasury collateral
→ liquidity returns when the trade unwinds
```

ON RRP is important because money-market funds cannot earn IORB. They need their own safe overnight
floor. When ON RRP balances fall, that can offset some of QT's reserve drain because cash leaves
the Fed facility and returns to private markets.

## Why the price knob works

Before 2008, reserves were scarce. Banks held very few excess reserves. Small changes in reserve
supply could move the **federal funds rate**, the overnight rate at which banks lend reserves to
each other.

After 2008, QE made reserves abundant. In January 2019, the FOMC said it would continue using a
regime with an **ample supply of reserves**, where short-rate control is exercised mainly by
administered rates rather than active daily reserve scarcity management.

The logic is simple:

```text
If a bank can earn IORB safely at the Fed,
it usually will not lend reserves to another private party at a much lower rate.
```

IORB becomes a floor for banks. ON RRP becomes a similar floor for eligible nonbanks such as
money-market funds. Together they help keep the federal funds rate, SOFR, repo rates, and T-bill
yields anchored near the Fed's short-rate stance.

**SOFR**, the Secured Overnight Financing Rate, is a broad overnight borrowing rate backed by
Treasury collateral. It is not the same as fed funds, but both are short-term funding rates.

## Where the separation breaks

The Fed can lower short rates while QT continues because price and quantity are separate knobs in
an ample-reserves system. But the separation has a boundary.

If QT drains reserves too far:

- banks become less comfortable with their settlement cash;
- repo and SOFR can jump;
- banks and dealers may scramble for overnight funding;
- the Fed may need to slow QT, stop QT, or inject reserves.

That is why “ample” does not mean “infinite.” It means enough reserves that the system does not
need to bid aggressively for them.

In October 2025, the FOMC said it would conclude reduction of aggregate securities holdings on
December 1. In December 2025, the FOMC said reserve balances had declined to levels consistent
with ample reserves and that it would initiate purchases of shorter-term Treasuries as needed to
maintain an ample supply of reserves.

## Why control the flow?

The Fed is trying to keep the financial system liquid enough to work, but not so loose that money
loses purchasing power or leverage becomes reckless.

Too little liquidity can break:

- payment settlement;
- repo funding;
- bank funding;
- credit availability;
- market-making capacity.

Too much liquidity can:

- compress risk premiums;
- encourage leverage;
- inflate asset prices;
- make inflation harder to control;
- reduce discipline in lending and investing.

The easiest analogy is a bathtub:

```text
Faucet = QE, repo, reserve injection
Drain = QT, ON RRP, TGA, currency demand
Water level = reserves and market liquidity
Thermostat = IORB and ON RRP policy rates
```

The analogy has a limit. Not all water reaches households or stocks directly. QE can make the
financial system more liquid, but it does not force banks to lend, households to spend, or stocks
to rise.

## Short rates and long rates are different machines

IORB and ON RRP strongly influence the short end: overnight rates, money-market yields, T-bills,
and floating-rate instruments.

The 10-year and 30-year Treasury yields are different. They reflect expected future short rates,
inflation, growth, Treasury supply, global demand, and **term premium**, which is the extra return
investors may require for holding long bonds instead of rolling short bills.

That is why a Fed cut does not guarantee mortgage rates fall. Mortgage rates usually care more
about the 10-year Treasury yield plus mortgage spreads than about today's fed funds rate.
