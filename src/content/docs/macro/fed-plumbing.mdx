## Start with the two knobs

The **Federal Reserve**, usually called the **Fed**, is the central bank of the United States.
It influences the economy through two related but different knobs:

```text
Price knob: what overnight money earns or costs
Quantity knob: how many reserves and securities sit in the financial system
```

The **FOMC**, or Federal Open Market Committee, is the Fed committee that announces the target
range for the **federal funds rate**. The federal funds rate is the overnight rate at which banks
lend reserve balances to each other.

**Reserves** are deposits that banks hold at the Fed. Think of them as the banking system's
settlement cash. Banks use reserves to settle payments, manage liquidity, and satisfy regulatory
needs.

## Why balance-sheet size is not the same as the policy rate

Before 2008, the Fed usually controlled the federal funds rate by making reserves scarce or
abundant in small daily operations. Today the system is closer to an **ample-reserves** framework:
there are enough reserves that the Fed can control short rates mainly by setting administered
rates.

An **administered rate** is a rate the Fed sets directly rather than a price discovered only in
the market. The two most important administered rates are:

- **IORB**, or interest on reserve balances: the rate the Fed pays banks on reserves.
- **ON RRP**, or overnight reverse repurchase agreement facility: a Fed facility where eligible
  money-market funds and other institutions can place cash overnight and receive a Fed-set rate.

If a bank can earn IORB safely at the Fed, it usually will not lend reserves to another private
counterparty at a much lower rate. If a money-market fund can use ON RRP, that facility gives it
a safer alternative to accepting very low private-market rates.

That is how the Fed can say:

```text
We want the overnight rate near this range,
even while our bond portfolio slowly shrinks.
```

## The balance sheet identity

The Fed's balance sheet has assets and liabilities.

**Assets** are what the Fed owns, mainly Treasury securities and agency mortgage-backed securities.
**Liabilities** are claims against the Fed, including bank reserves, paper currency, the Treasury's
account, and ON RRP balances.

A simplified identity is:

```text
Fed assets
= bank reserves
+ currency in circulation
+ Treasury General Account
+ ON RRP
+ other liabilities and capital
```

The **Treasury General Account**, or **TGA**, is the U.S. Treasury's checking account at the Fed.
When the Treasury builds up this account, cash leaves the private banking system and bank reserves
can fall.

The important reserve-change shortcut is:

```text
Change in reserves
≈ change in Fed assets
- change in currency
- change in TGA
- change in ON RRP
- other liability changes
```

## Numeric example: QT does not always drain reserves one-for-one

**QT**, or quantitative tightening, means the Fed lets securities mature or run off without fully
replacing them. This shrinks Fed assets.

Suppose one month looks like this:

| Item | Change | What it does to reserves |
|---|---:|---|
| Fed securities runoff | `-$60B` | drains reserves |
| ON RRP balance falls | `-$40B` | adds reserves back |
| TGA rises | `+$30B` | drains reserves |
| currency rises | `+$10B` | drains reserves |

The rough reserve change is:

```text
-$60B runoff
+ $40B ON RRP decline
- $30B TGA increase
- $10B currency increase
= -$60B reserves
```

So the Fed balance sheet shrank by `$60B`, but the reserve effect depended on other liability
movements. If ON RRP had fallen by `$80B` instead of `$40B`, reserves might have fallen much less.

## How can rates stay controlled during QT?

The answer is: QT changes the **quantity** of reserves, while IORB and ON RRP help set the
**price** of overnight money.

This works as long as reserves remain **ample**. Ample does not mean infinite. It means banks have
enough settlement cash that overnight funding markets do not need to bid aggressively for scarce
reserves.

Watch for signs that reserves are becoming less ample:

- **SOFR**, the Secured Overnight Financing Rate, rises meaningfully relative to the Fed's target
  range. SOFR is an overnight borrowing rate backed by Treasury collateral.
- repo rates become volatile near quarter-end or tax-payment dates;
- the effective federal funds rate trades persistently high in the target range;
- banks and dealers show funding stress;
- the Fed slows QT or increases reserve-management operations.
