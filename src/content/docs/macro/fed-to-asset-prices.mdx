## The point is transmission, not prediction

The Fed does not directly set the price of every stock, bond, house, or currency. It changes the
price and availability of money. Investors then reprice assets through several channels.

The job of a value investor is not to guess the next macro headline. It is to ask:

```text
Which part of this asset's value is exposed to rates, liquidity, inflation, or financing?
```

## Five transmission channels

**Discount-rate channel.** A discount rate is the required return used to convert future cash flows
into today's value. Higher required returns lower the present value of distant cash flows.

**Financing-cost channel.** Financing cost is what households, companies, and investors pay to
borrow. Mortgages, corporate debt, margin loans, construction loans, and refinancing all transmit
rate changes into real decisions.

**Liquidity channel.** Liquidity means the ability to raise cash or trade without large price
damage. Reserves, repo markets, and credit availability influence risk appetite and forced selling.

**Inflation/cost channel.** Inflation means broad purchasing-power erosion. Energy, wages,
insurance, materials, and rents can change company margins and household budgets.

**Portfolio-allocation channel.** When cash and T-bills pay more, risky assets must justify why
they deserve capital. When safe yields fall, investors may accept lower prospective returns
elsewhere.

## Define the asset classes before comparing them

**Cash / T-bills** are short-term lending to the U.S. government. They are usually the opportunity
cost baseline: what you can earn while waiting.

**Treasury bonds** are longer-term lending to the U.S. government. Their prices move opposite to
yields. A 10-year bond with duration around `8` loses roughly `8%` if yields rise `1%`.

**Corporate bonds / credit** are loans to companies. Their yield equals a Treasury yield plus a
**credit spread**, which is extra compensation for default and liquidity risk.

**Stocks** are residual ownership claims. Stock owners are paid after employees, suppliers, taxes,
lenders, and preferred claims. Stock value depends on business cash flows and the return investors
require.

**Growth stocks** are stocks where more value sits in distant future cash flows. That makes them
more sensitive to discount-rate changes.

**Value or cyclical stocks** often have more near-term earnings but more operating sensitivity to
recessions, commodity cycles, or credit conditions.

**Banks and insurers** are financial companies whose economics depend on the yield curve, deposit
costs, credit losses, and investment income. For insurers, **float** is money held before claims
are paid.

**REITs** are real-estate investment trusts: companies that own income-producing real estate and
trade like stocks. They are sensitive to rent growth, debt costs, and **cap rates**, which are
property income yields.

**New homes** are newly built houses sold by builders. Demand depends on mortgage affordability,
builder incentives, construction financing, land costs, and supply.

**Used homes** are existing homes sold by owners. A seller with a low old mortgage can refuse to
sell, so high rates may freeze supply before prices fully adjust.

**Commodities / oil** are physical inputs. Oil shocks can raise inflation and help energy producers
while hurting consumers and rate-sensitive assets.

**Currencies** are relative prices between money systems. Rate differentials, inflation, trade
balances, and capital flows affect exchange rates.

## Housing is where rates become painfully concrete

A 30-year mortgage is a long loan secured by a house. When the mortgage rate rises from `5.5%` to
`6.5%`, the same house price requires a higher monthly payment. If income does not rise too, the
buyer can afford less house.

This is why new homes and used homes must be separated:

- A **builder** can cut price, buy down the buyer's rate, offer upgrades, or slow construction.
- A **used-home owner** can keep the old low-rate mortgage and not sell.

High rates can therefore reduce transaction volume before they reduce reported home prices.

## Connect this to the 1970s oil shock

The 1970s are useful because they show why the numerator and denominator can both move against
investors.

In the 1973 oil shock, oil moved from roughly `$3` per barrel to nearly `$12` by 1974. In the
1979/1980 shock, oil moved above `$35` and near `$40` per barrel. Energy became a cost shock for
households and businesses. Inflation expectations rose. The Fed eventually tightened severely;
the Volcker-era federal funds rate reached roughly `20%` in 1981.

That combination hurt many assets through both sides of valuation:

```text
Numerator pressure: weaker real demand, higher costs, lower margins
Denominator pressure: higher required returns and tighter money
```

Today does not have to replay the 1970s. The U.S. economy is less oil-intensive, the Fed has an
explicit inflation-fighting framework, and the monetary system now uses an ample-reserves
operating regime. But oil shocks still matter because they can squeeze consumers, raise headline
inflation, and force investors to question whether current yields compensate them enough.

## Practical investor checklist

When a macro shock appears, do not ask only "bullish or bearish?" Ask:

1. Does it change the company's cash flows?
2. Does it change the discount rate or market multiple?
3. Does it change customer financing or demand?
4. Does it change credit spreads or refinancing risk?
5. Does it change safe cash yields enough to raise the opportunity-cost hurdle?
6. Does it create a temporary price move or a permanent impairment?
