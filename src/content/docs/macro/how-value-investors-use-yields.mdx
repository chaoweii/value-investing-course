## Use yields as a discipline, not a prophecy

A value investor does not need to forecast the next Federal Reserve meeting. The practical job
is to understand what the current price assumes and ensure the investment remains acceptable
across plausible yield regimes.

## Four disciplined uses

### 1. Set opportunity cost

Begin with the real return available from safe assets, then demand compensation for equity risk,
uncertainty, illiquidity, and analytical error. Do not mechanically add a fixed spread forever;
ask what risk you are actually underwriting.

### 2. Reverse the valuation

Instead of selecting a discount rate to produce a desired value, ask:

- What return does today’s price imply?
- What growth and margin path must occur?
- Would the stock still clear your hurdle if yields stayed higher for longer?

### 3. Build regimes, not point forecasts

| Regime | Possible cause | Underwriting response |
|---|---|---|
| Higher yields, healthy growth | Productivity and resilient demand | Raise hurdle; do not automatically cut cash-flow forecasts |
| Higher yields, inflation/fiscal stress | Purchasing-power and term-premium concerns | Raise hurdle and pressure-test margins and multiples |
| Lower yields, soft landing | Disinflation with durable demand | Revisit valuation without assuming a boom |
| Lower yields, recession | Flight to safety and weak growth | Cut operating forecasts; do not celebrate the lower denominator |

### 4. Demand a larger margin of safety when sensitivity is hidden

The correct response to rate uncertainty is rarely a precise macro bet. It is usually:

- a valuation sensitivity table;
- a more conservative scenario probability;
- a smaller position;
- a requirement that more value come from near-term owner cash flow.

higher required returns. If the margin of safety disappears under plausible regimes, the stock
is not robustly cheap.
