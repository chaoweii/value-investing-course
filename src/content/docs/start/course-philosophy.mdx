You already know how markets feel. You have watched prices move, formed theses, and
experienced the tension between conviction and uncertainty. This course does not
start by pretending you are a beginner. It changes the object of attention:

> From predicting the next quote to underwriting the next decade of owner economics.

## Six operating rules

1. **Integrity is the first management gate.** Minority owners entrust capital to people they
   cannot directly supervise.
2. **A good business is not automatically a good investment.** Price determines return.
3. **Every material claim has a type.** Reported fact, derived calculation, assumption,
   or judgment must never blur into one another.
4. **Owner FCF is per-share economics.** SBC, buybacks, dilution, working capital,
   maintenance capex, and owner cash all matter.
5. **The bear case is a mechanism, not a smaller number.** Describe what breaks and
   how that failure reaches revenue, cost, cash flow, and valuation.
6. **Opportunity cost is part of valuation.** A stock must justify why it deserves
   capital instead of BRK.B, the S&P 500, the short-term risk-free Treasury yield,
   or the next best idea. In portfolio discussions, “cash” normally means Treasury
   bills or a government money-market fund earning approximately that yield—not
   idle currency earning nothing. This return is nominally low-risk in U.S. dollars,
   but inflation can still reduce its purchasing power.

## Durable lessons and live decisions

The course separates two clocks:

- **Durable lessons** teach mechanisms that should survive changing prices.
- **Live companions** show dated valuation outputs imported from the workbench.

Never let a dated verdict quietly become a permanent principle.
