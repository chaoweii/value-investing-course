## Phase -1 · Understand what you own

Learn the compact legal and economic foundation beneath every later lesson:

1. The corporation owns the business assets; shareholders own shares in the corporation.
2. Common equity is a residual claim and usually receives value after senior claims.
3. Limited liability normally caps your loss at invested capital, but does not protect that capital.
4. Dividends and buybacks are capital-allocation choices, not automatic shareholder entitlements.
5. ADRs, VIEs, dual-class shares, preferred shares, and tracking stocks change the ownership bargain.

Use the [Capital Stack And Shareholder Claim Lab](/ownership/capital-stack/) before making the
opening decisions.

## Phase 0 · Decide before theory

Make an initial decision on TOST, ADYEY, and BRK.B. Each company exposes a different
failure mode:

| Company | The tempting shortcut | The harder owner question |
|---|---|---|
| TOST | “Owners love the product.” | Does that love become durable owner FCF per share at this price? |
| ADYEY | “The stock fell and the company is high quality.” | Does the downside remain controlled if growth and margins disappoint? |
| BRK.B | “It is safe, so it must be attractive.” | What return can its scale and capital allocation produce from today’s price? |

## Phase 1 · Think like a business owner

1. From trading symbols to owning businesses.
2. Evidence hierarchy and research audit trails.
3. Financial statements, owner earnings, SBC, buybacks, and owner cash.
4. Moats through demand durability, pricing power, switching costs, and expansion cost.
5. Management incentives and capital allocation.

## Phase 2 · Underwrite value and returns

Before valuation, complete the [Macro Backbone · The Price Of Money](/macro/). It explains
how Treasury yields connect inflation, growth, financing, opportunity cost, and the present
value of owner cash flow.

Toast becomes the longitudinal build-along case in this phase. You will repeatedly return to the
same chain: restaurant value, recurring gross profit, operating leverage, owner FCF, dilution,
valuation, and position size. ADYEY and BRK.B then show where the method must change.

1. Revenue-and-cost driver models.
2. Product scale, profit inflection, ROIC, reinvestment runway, and margin expansion.
3. DCF, SOTP, and current-asset treatment.
4. Scenarios, IRR, margin of safety, recognition timing, and benchmark opportunity cost.
5. Falsifiers, conservative audits, sizing, and monitoring.

## Phase 3 · Build pattern recognition

Compare payments, networks, software, semiconductors, allocators, builders, consumer
brands, and experience-economy platforms. The goal is not to memorize ratios. It is
to learn which economic mechanism deserves which valuation method.

## Phase 4 · Construct the long-term portfolio

Turn individual underwriting into a survivable allocation. Learn to stack favorable asymmetry,
control permanent loss and hidden correlation, and correct mistakes when evidence changes without
turning a tax-free account into a frequent-trading account.

## Phase 5 · Understand the market machine

Learn how primary issuance, secondary trading, market makers, ETFs, index rules, derivatives, and
forced flows convert ownership claims into quoted prices. Use this knowledge to recognize
temporary mechanical pressure without confusing it with a permanent change in business value.
