## The situation

Berkshire is not merely a collection of operating companies and public securities.
Its economic system combines insurance float, wholly owned businesses, tax-efficient
capital movement, liquidity, and a willingness to wait.

That makes it a useful benchmark even when it is not the most attractive stock:
every new investment should explain why it deserves capital instead of a diversified,
liquid allocator with a resilient balance sheet.

## The allocator equation

```text
Operating earnings
+ underwriting profit or loss
+ returns earned on float and retained capital
+ value created by acquisitions and repurchases
− cost of mistakes, taxes, and idle capital
= growth in per-share intrinsic value
```

The important unit is **per-share intrinsic value**, not reported revenue, total
assets, or the absolute size of the investment portfolio.

## What makes float valuable?

Float is not automatically free money. Its economic value depends on:

1. The duration before claims are paid.
2. The underwriting profit or cost incurred while holding it.
3. The returns available on the assets funded by it.
4. The liquidity and regulatory constraints attached to it.

## Falsifiers

- Insurance underwriting becomes structurally unprofitable.
- Succession weakens capital-allocation discipline or operating autonomy.
- Scale forces persistent low-return deployment or excessive idle cash.
- Repurchases or acquisitions occur above conservative intrinsic value.
- The expected per-share return fails to clear simpler alternatives.

<ExerciseReveal prompt="When should a faster-growing company fail the BRK.B opportunity-cost test?">
  When its growth requires a high entry multiple, fragile assumptions, repeated dilution,
  or a bear case with permanent capital loss. Growth is only valuable when the investor
  captures enough of it per share at the price paid.

- How much value should be assigned to liquidity and crisis optionality?
- Is the cash balance a strategic asset or evidence of limited reinvestment runway?
- How would you value the company without relying on a single book-value multiple?
- What specific advantage must another stock offer to justify greater risk?
