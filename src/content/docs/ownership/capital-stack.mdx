## The payment waterfall

When a company is liquidated, common shareholders are generally last. A simplified ordering is:

```text
Asset value
− liquidation costs
− employees, suppliers, and taxes
− secured lenders and other debt claims
− preferred-stock claims
= residual value available to common shareholders
```

Contract terms and applicable law can change the exact ordering. The durable idea is that
**enterprise assets support many claims; common equity receives only the remainder**.

<ChineseNote title="有资产，不代表普通股一定有价值">
企业可能拥有大量资产，但如果债权和其他优先索取权更大，普通股仍可能归零。普通股是最后一层缓冲。
</ChineseNote>

## Connect this to valuation

An operating-business valuation often starts with enterprise value. To reach common-equity value,
the analyst must account for debt, non-controlling interests, preferred claims, and genuinely
excess owner cash. To reach per-share value, divide by the correct diluted share count.

```text
Market capitalization
+ debt
+ preferred stock and non-controlling interests
− genuinely excess cash
= approximate enterprise value
```

This is why “the company has cash” is incomplete analysis. Ask who controls it, what obligations
it supports, whether it is excess, and how many claims share the remainder.

## Why markets normally quote market cap

**Market capitalization** is the market value of the common equity:

```text
Current share price × common shares outstanding = market capitalization
```

It is normally reported because common shares are what public-market investors directly trade.
Market cap tells you:

- what all common shareholders' claims cost together;
- how large your position is relative to the listed equity;
- how the company is weighted in many stock indexes;
- and the starting value for per-share returns.

**Enterprise value** is not another traded security. It is an analytical estimate of the operating
business value available to all capital providers. It must be calculated, and analysts can disagree
about which cash is excess, which liabilities resemble debt, and which non-controlling claims to
include.

<ChineseNote title="市值是你买的股权价格，EV 是整个经营资产的估值视角">
股票投资者直接买卖普通股，所以市场最常显示市值。EV 用来比较企业经营价值，并把债务、优先权和超额现金纳入考虑。两者回答的问题不同，不能互相替代。
</ChineseNote>

## When market cap misleads

Imagine two restaurant corporations with identical `$500` market caps:

|  | Restaurant A | Restaurant B |
|---|---:|---:|
| Market cap | `$500` | `$500` |
| Debt | `$0` | `$700` |
| Excess cash | `$200` | `$0` |
| Approximate EV | `$300` | `$1,200` |

Their shares have the same quoted market value, but investors are not assigning the same value to
the operating businesses. Restaurant B's operations must support both the `$500` common-equity
claim and `$700` debt claim. Restaurant A's shareholders also have `$200` of excess cash behind
their claim.

This leads to the matching rule:

| Valuation question | Appropriate value | Match with |
|---|---|---|
| What do common shareholders collectively pay? | Market cap or equity value | Net income, equity FCF, per-share earnings |
| What is the operating business worth before financing? | Enterprise value | Revenue, EBITDA, operating cash flow before debt claims |

Do not divide enterprise value by net income, or compare market cap with EBITDA, without carefully
reconciling who owns the numerator and who receives the denominator.

## Two valid intrinsic-value paths

The correct path depends on whose cash flow you forecast:

```text
Equity owner FCF
→ discount at the required equity return
= operating equity value attributable to common shareholders
+ separately excluded owner cash, if applicable
= intrinsic equity value
÷ diluted shares
= intrinsic value per share
```

```text
FCFF available to debt and equity
→ discount at WACC
= intrinsic enterprise value
− debt, preferred claims, and NCI
+ genuinely excess owner cash
= intrinsic equity value
÷ diluted shares
= intrinsic value per share
```

Never subtract debt again after valuing cash flow that already belongs only to common shareholders.
Never add cash twice if it already generates forecast cash flow.

## Examples that choose different paths

| Company type | Primary lens | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Cash-rich, debt-light TOST | Equity owner FCF | The common-share claim is the main valuation question; EV is a useful operating cross-check. |
| Leveraged restaurant | FCFF-to-EV plus equity reconciliation | Debt holders own a material part of operating value. |
| Cash-rich mature company | Both equity value and ex-cash EV | Market cap may look expensive while the operating business is reasonably priced. |
| Debt-heavy company | EV and debt-service analysis | A low market cap can hide an expensive or fragile operating business. |
| Bank, insurer, or Berkshire | Equity valuation or SOTP | Cash and debt may be operating raw materials rather than simple excess cash and financing. |
| Acquisition target | EV | A buyer acquires operations and assumes, repays, or refinances capital claims. |

<ExerciseReveal prompt="Two companies have identical revenue, EBITDA, and market caps. One has no debt; the other has substantial debt. Are their operating businesses valued equally?">
No. The debt-heavy company's enterprise value is higher because debt holders also have a claim on
the operating business. Market cap compares only the common-equity claims and hides that difference.
