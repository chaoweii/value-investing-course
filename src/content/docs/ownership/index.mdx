Before deciding whether a stock is attractive, understand what the stock gives you.
A share is not a miniature bag containing part of the company's cash, buildings, or products.
It is a legal and economic claim on a corporation.

That claim can be extraordinarily valuable. It is also limited, indirect, and paid after many
other claims.

<ChineseNote title="先看清你买了什么">
买入股票，不是把公司现金按比例装进你的口袋。你买到的是公司股权中的剩余索取权；公司先履行其他义务，剩下的价值才属于普通股股东。
</ChineseNote>

## The durable principle

The business belongs to the corporation. Managers operate it under the board's oversight.
Common shareholders usually elect directors and hold the residual economic claim. They do not
directly control daily operations or possess the corporation's assets.

In practice, an individual public-market investor rarely owns enough votes to change the board.
Large funds may collectively hold much of a company, but each asset manager votes separately on
behalf of its fund investors. They often support management on routine matters, while occasionally
opposing directors, compensation, or governance practices. Headline institutional ownership
therefore does not guarantee active or effective shareholder control.

## A useful mental model: productive capacity working for you

Treat each share as hiring a tiny fraction of the company's productive system to work on your
behalf. Its people, products, customer relationships, and capital may create value while you sleep.
But unlike hiring an employee directly, a minority shareholder has very little power over daily
decisions.

You therefore entrust management with the corporation's cash, reputation, reinvestment choices,
disclosures, and treatment of minority owners. **Integrity comes before intelligence** because a
capable manager who cannot be trusted can redirect the value created by the business away from
shareholders.

<ChineseNote title="把股份想成替你工作的生产系统">
每股像是一小部分替你工作的企业生产能力，但你几乎不能直接指挥它。因此，管理层诚信不是加分项，而是股东权利能否真正转化为价值的基础。
</ChineseNote>

These lessons describe typical U.S. public-company common shares. Exact rights depend on the
company's charter, bylaws, share class, jurisdiction, custody structure, and applicable law.
They are education, not legal advice.
