## Read the security, not only the company name

| Structure | What changes | Question to ask |
|---|---|---|
| Voting and non-voting shares | Economic exposure may be similar while one class has no ordinary vote. | Am I deliberately accepting economics without a governance voice? |
| Dual-class shares | One class may have one vote, fewer votes, or many more votes per share. | Who controls the board despite owning less of the economics? |
| ADR or ADS | A depositary receipt represents a stated number or fraction of foreign ordinary shares. | What are the ratio, fees, FX exposure, custody rights, and cancellation risks? |
| VIE exposure | Investors may own an offshore contractual claim rather than direct equity in operating assets. | Are contracts enforceable and can value reach the listed security? |
| Preferred stock | Priority, dividends, and conversion terms differ from common equity. | What is senior, fixed, callable, or convertible? |
| Tracking stock | Economics track a business or group without necessarily owning its assets directly. | Which liabilities, governance rights, and capital-allocation powers sit elsewhere? |

<ChineseNote title="股票代码相同，不代表权利结构简单">
分析公司之前，先读清你实际买入的证券。ADR、VIE、双重股权和追踪股都可能让“拥有公司”这句话变得更间接。
</ChineseNote>

## Economic ownership and voting control are different

Two share classes can participate similarly in the company's economic results while carrying
radically different voting power. Always calculate both:

```text
Economic ownership = your economic shares ÷ total economic shares
Voting control = votes attached to your shares ÷ total votes outstanding
```

Dual-class structures are not automatically bad. They can protect a capable founder from
short-term pressure. They can also leave public shareholders unable to replace poor leadership,
oppose self-interested transactions, or influence capital allocation.

The correct question is not merely “Do I have a vote?” It is:

> If management stops deserving trust, can outside shareholders meaningfully change anything?

## Berkshire: unequal votes without founder super-voting

| Berkshire security | Approximate economic relationship | Approximate voting relationship | Conversion |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| `BRK.A` | One Class A share | One Class A vote unit | Holder may convert one A into 1,500 B shares |
| `BRK.B` | 1/1,500 of one Class A share | 1/10,000 of one Class A vote | Cannot convert B into A |

BRK.B therefore receives substantially less voting power than its proportional economic interest.
For example, 1,500 BRK.B shares approximate the economics of one BRK.A share, but together carry
only about 15% of one BRK.A share's voting power.

This structure matters, but Berkshire's investment case has historically relied heavily on
management trust and capital-allocation culture rather than minority voting influence.

## Alphabet: public voting, public non-voting, and insider super-voting

| Alphabet security | Publicly traded? | Votes per share | Practical meaning |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| `GOOGL`, Class A | Yes | 1 | Public investors receive an ordinary voting voice |
| Class B | No; primarily insiders | 10 | Super-voting shares concentrate control with founders and insiders |
| `GOOG`, Class C | Yes | 0 | Public investors receive economic exposure without an ordinary vote |

GOOG and GOOGL may have similar economic exposure, but they are not the same governance security.
The founders' Class B votes mean that even GOOGL holders collectively may have limited ability to
change control.

<ChineseNote title="经济权利相似，投票权可以完全不同">
BRK.B 的经济份额与投票份额并不同比例；Alphabet 的 GOOGL 每股一票，GOOG 没有普通投票权，而内部 Class B 每股十票。买入前要同时计算经济所有权和投票控制权。
</ChineseNote>

## Do most technology stocks have no voting rights?

No. Many listed technology companies still issue ordinary one-vote common shares. However,
founder-led technology companies disproportionately use dual-class structures, and some offer
public non-voting shares. The important habit is to inspect the exact ticker and share class
rather than generalize from the industry.

<ExerciseReveal prompt="GOOG and GOOGL provide similar exposure to Alphabet's business. Are they the same security?">
No. GOOGL Class A normally carries one vote per share. GOOG Class C normally carries no ordinary
vote. Alphabet insiders also hold Class B super-voting shares with ten votes each, so buying
GOOGL provides a vote without necessarily providing meaningful control.
</ExerciseReveal>

## Opening-case contrasts

### TOST

TOST common shareholders own a residual claim after operating obligations. SBC can issue new
claims, while buybacks spend company cash to retire claims. Balance-sheet cash remains corporate
property until capital allocation makes it available to owners.

### ADYEY

ADYEY is an ADR. Each ADS represents a fraction of an Adyen ordinary share, so valuation must
respect the ADS ratio, EUR operating economics, USD market price, FX, and depositary structure.

### BRK.B

BRK.B and BRK.A differ in price, voting power, and conversion mechanics. One BRK.B share represents
approximately 1/1,500 of a BRK.A share's economic interest but only 1/10,000 of its voting power.
Similar exposure to Berkshire's economics does not mean identical governance rights.

### Future contrast cases

PDD's VIE exposure and FWONK's tracking-stock structure are reminders that a listed security can
