## Three things that sound similar

<OwnershipRelationshipMap />

- The **business** is the economic activity: serving customers, paying employees, and earning cash.
- The **corporation** is the legal entity that owns assets, signs contracts, owes liabilities, and operates the business.
- A **share** is a unit of ownership in that corporation.

The distinction matters because one corporation can own several businesses, and a business can be
sold from one corporation to another. The corporation is the legally recognized owner and
contracting party. The business is the productive activity happening inside that legal container.

Owning 10% of a restaurant corporation does not permit you to take 10% of its ovens, withdraw
10% of its bank account, or tell the chef what to cook. The corporation owns those assets.
Your shares give you an indirect economic claim and whatever governance rights attach to the class.

<ChineseNote title="公司财产不是股东个人财产">

</ChineseNote>

## The residual claim

Common shareholders are <GlossaryTerm term="residual claimants" definition="Owners entitled to value remaining after higher-priority obligations are satisfied." />.
Value reaches them only after the corporation meets its obligations. It may arrive through:

- dividends declared by the board;
- cash-funded repurchases that reduce shares outstanding;
- acquisition proceeds after claims are settled;
- or a higher future share price because expected per-share owner cash increased.

No route is automatic. A profitable company can retain cash indefinitely, invest it badly, or
issue enough new shares that each existing share owns a smaller fraction.

## You hire the system, but cannot manage it

A productive mental model is that buying a share hires a tiny fraction of the company to work for
you. You supply capital today because you expect the business system to create more value later.

The analogy has an important limit: you cannot normally direct that system. As a minority owner,
you have little leverage over pricing, hiring, acquisitions, reinvestment, disclosures, or
capital returns. You usually vote for directors, then rely on the board and management.

That creates an unavoidable trust problem:

```text
Business quality
× management competence
× management integrity
× fair treatment of minority owners
= value that may actually reach each share
```

Integrity is the first gate. Skill without integrity can make the company larger while making
the minority shareholder's claim worse.
