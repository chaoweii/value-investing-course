## Limited liability

For an ordinary public-company shareholder, company debts are normally not personal debts.
If you invest $1,000 and the company fails, your ordinary maximum loss is the $1,000 invested.
Creditors generally cannot demand that you personally repay the corporation's loans.

This protection is called **limited liability**. Exceptions such as personal guarantees, fraud,
or piercing the corporate veil generally concern controlling or private owners, not an ordinary
public-market shareholder.

## How the claim can weaken

- **Business failure:** revenue and cash flow deteriorate.
- **Bankruptcy:** senior claimants consume all available value.
- **Dilution:** new shares make each existing share a smaller ownership fraction.
- **Poor capital allocation:** management spends retained cash below its opportunity cost.
- **Weak governance:** controlling shareholders or managers act against minority owners.
- **Structural distance:** ADRs, VIEs, and tracking stocks may provide weaker or indirect claims.
- **Overpayment:** a wonderful business produces a poor return because the purchase price was too high.

Limited liability caps the ordinary dollar loss. It does not guarantee liquidity, voting influence,
dividends, fair treatment, or an adequate investment return.

It protected the investor from owing the company's debts. It did not protect the invested capital.

</ExerciseReveal>
