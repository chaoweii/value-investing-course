## Typical rights

An ordinary U.S. public-company common shareholder will often have the right to:

- vote for directors and on certain major corporate actions, subject to share class;
- receive a proportional share of a dividend **after the board declares it**;
- receive a proportional share of residual liquidation value after senior claims;
- sell or transfer shares, subject to market liquidity and applicable restrictions;
- receive required public disclosures and participate in the proxy process;
- pursue limited legal remedies when duties are breached.

These rights do not mean shareholders run the business. Management makes daily decisions. The
board oversees management. Shareholders normally influence the system indirectly by electing
directors and voting on a limited set of matters.

## One share does not always mean one vote

An ordinary voting common share often carries one vote. But the company's charter can create
multiple classes with different voting rights:

- **one-vote shares:** one share normally casts one vote;
- **non-voting shares:** receive economic exposure but generally cannot vote;
- **low-vote shares:** vote, but with less power than another class;
- **super-voting shares:** may cast 10 or more votes per share.

Voting power is therefore a separate calculation from economic ownership:

```text
Economic ownership = shares owned ÷ total economic shares
Voting control = votes attached to shares owned ÷ total votes
```

A founder can own a minority of the company's economics while retaining majority voting control.
The detailed [ownership-structures lesson](/ownership/ownership-structures/) compares Berkshire
and Alphabet.

## Why large institutional ownership may still provide weak oversight

Your intuition is broadly correct, with several distinctions:

1. **Individuals are usually dispersed.** A small shareholder's vote is legally real but rarely
   decisive by itself.
2. **Funds manage shares for beneficiaries.** Vanguard, BlackRock, pension funds, and active
   managers generally vote shares held by their funds; the asset manager is not the ultimate
   economic owner of all those assets.
3. **Institutions do not act as one owner.** Several funds may collectively own a majority, but
   they have different mandates, policies, incentives, and votes.
4. **Routine support is not always negligence.** Director elections and ordinary proposals may
   deserve support. Institutions also engage privately, so disagreement is not always visible in
   the final vote.
5. **The incentive problem is real.** Deep governance research is costly, while its benefit is
   shared with every shareholder. Index funds cannot sell a company merely because governance is
   poor, active managers may prefer selling to fighting, and commercial relationships can create
   conflicts.

This produces a classic **principal-agent chain**:

```text
Beneficial owner
→ fund or account
→ asset manager and stewardship team
→ board of directors
→ management
→ operating business
```

At every step, the person making decisions may have different incentives from the ultimate owner.
Voting rights matter, but they are not a substitute for trustworthy management, a capable board,
aligned incentives, and a sensible purchase price.

## Why integrity is part of the investment

Minority shareholders usually see the business through information selected and organized by
management. They also rely on management to allocate retained earnings, price acquisitions,
issue or repurchase shares, set compensation, and disclose bad news promptly.

Before admiring a manager's vision, ask:

- Are setbacks described as plainly as successes?
- Does compensation reward per-share value creation or merely company size?
- Are related-party transactions and conflicts handled fairly?
- Do buybacks occur below intrinsic value, or merely offset generous dilution?
- Does management keep promises and explain changed decisions honestly?
- Are minority shareholders treated as partners or as a financing source?

Trust is not blind faith. It is a conclusion built from repeated behavior, incentives, governance,
and evidence.

## Rights you usually do not have

- You cannot force the company to declare a dividend.
- You cannot demand that the company repurchase your shares.
- You cannot withdraw your proportional share of company cash.
- You cannot personally direct employees or sign contracts for the corporation.
- You are not guaranteed equal voting power: some share classes have little or no vote.

<ExerciseReveal prompt="The company earned a large profit this year. Must it pay you a dividend?">
No. Unless a dividend is declared, common shareholders generally cannot demand one. The board may
retain the cash, reinvest it, repay debt, acquire another company, or repurchase shares.
</ExerciseReveal>
