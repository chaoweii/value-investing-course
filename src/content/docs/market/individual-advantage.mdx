## Your advantage is freedom, not speed

An individual investor usually has no obligation to:

- buy an expensive index addition;
- sell a deleted constituent at the rebalance close;
- remain fully invested;
- match a quarterly benchmark;
- own only large liquid securities;
- or explain temporary underperformance to clients.

You can hold Treasury bills, investigate off-index companies, wait for evidence, and provide
liquidity when rule-bound holders must transact.

## Mechanical-flow decision playbook

1. **Identify the forced participant.** What rule, redemption, hedge, or collateral call requires action?
2. **Quantify the likely need.** Compare required shares with average volume and free float.
3. **Separate exposure from physical shares.** Can derivatives or creations satisfy the mandate?
4. **Separate temporary flow from business value.** Does the event change owner cash flow?
5. **Demand a valuation advantage.** Being contrarian is not enough.
6. **Write the falsifier and review date.** Do not let an event trade quietly become a permanent thesis.

<ExerciseReveal prompt="An index deletion falls 12% on the rebalance date. Is it automatically attractive?">
No. Mechanical selling may create opportunity, but deletion can also reflect deteriorating size,
liquidity, governance, or business performance. Re-underwrite the company and compare intrinsic
value with the new market offer before acting.
</ExerciseReveal>
